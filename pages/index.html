Meter- to Millimeter Emission from Cool Stellar Systems:

Latest Results, Synergies Across the Spectrum, and Outlook for the Next Decade

Lead organizers: Jan Forbrich (Hertfordshire), Peter K. G. Williams (Harvard CfA). Co-organizers: Edo Berger (Harvard CfA), Manual Güdel (Vienna), Rachel Osten (STScI).

 

A splinter session of the Cool Stars 20 conference to be held July 29–August 3, 2018 in Boston, USA.

Radio observations of cool stellar systems provide unique information on their magnetic fields, high-energy processes, and chemistry. Buoyed by powerful new instruments (e.g. ALMA, JVLA, LOFAR), advances in related fields (e.g. the Gaia astrometric revolution), and above all a renewed interest in the relevant stellar astrophysics, stellar radio astronomy is experiencing a renaissance. In this splinter session, participants will take stock of the present state of stellar radio astronomy and chart a course for the field’s future. Read more on the Motivation page.

Format of the Session

Target audience
Astrophysicists interested in the formation, magnetism, activity, chemistry, and/or habitability of cool stars and their environs. While this splinter connects these topics through the theme of long-wavelength stellar emission, prior expertise in radio astronomy is emphatically not expected of attendees — a major goal of the splinter is to enhance the dialogue between radio specialists and the broader community of stellar astronomers!
Total duration
Three hours.
Introduction
Start off with an overview of the science of long-wavelength stellar emission, aimed at non-specialists.
Contributed talks
Connecting to the themes motivated above. We are particularly interested in talks that will connect long-wavelength stellar emission to other regions of the EM spectrum as well as a mixture of observational and theoretical approaches. See above for the form to submit a contributed talk.
“Unconference” style
Ample time will be reserved for discussion, letting the topics of interest emerge organically from the structured portions of the session.
Forward-looking
Although the discussion topics will (hopefully!) range widely, we expect to conclude with an outlook on upcoming observatories and their role in the upcoming US Decadal Survey. After having heard the latest results from observational capabilities that are still new, the session thus will end on a forward-looking note to identify the most exciting science questions and to strategize the advocacy that will ensure that these questions get answered in a resource-constrained environment.
